Three patients. One platform. Different reasons to book.

These personas were built from Opencare's internal product roadmap, behavioral archetypes documented by the design team, and a dataset of 350,000 patient surveys and 33,000 real appointments. All three are first-time patients on Opencare, but their journeys, motivations, and needs are meaningfully different.

Persona 01 · The Insurance Maximizer

Marcus Chen

A methodical, cost-conscious professional who won't book a dentist without first confirming they're in-network — and will compare three practices before committing to one.

Age 34
Location Chicago, IL
Role Project Manager, Tech Startup
Tech Proficiency High
Last Dental Visit 14 months ago
01 · Context & Background

Marcus is a first-generation immigrant in a fast-paced product role. He has employer-sponsored PPO dental insurance through his company and is acutely aware of his annual benefit limits — he knows he has a $1,500 maximum and hasn't used any of it this calendar year.

He's used to doing research before making any purchase decision and applies that same rigor to healthcare. He's comfortable with digital tools and expects them to surface the information he needs without requiring extra legwork on his part.

No significant accessibility needs. He's bilingual (English, Mandarin) but navigates healthcare entirely in English.

02 · Tool Stack

Relies heavily on mobile-first experiences. Tends to start searches on Google, then moves to review platforms like Yelp or Zocdoc to validate options before booking.

Google Search Yelp / Zocdoc Delta Dental Portal iPhone / Safari Google Maps Email (Gmail)
Insight from product data: 63% of Opencare users select "accepts my insurance" as their #1 filter. Marcus represents this majority.
03 · Communication Style
Tone
Direct & neutral
Style
Concise, task-oriented
Language
Layman's terms
Channel pref.
Email > SMS

Marcus writes short, factual messages. He'll ask one specific question per message and expects a direct answer. He dislikes ambiguity and will disengage if responses are vague or full of caveats.

04 · Goals & Motivations
  • 1 Find a dentist that accepts Delta Dental PPO so he pays nothing or near-nothing out of pocket for his annual cleaning.
  • 2 Use his remaining annual benefit before it resets in January — this creates urgency in Q4.
  • 3 Find a practice within a short commute from his office so he can book a lunchtime slot.
  • 4 Validate quality through reviews and ratings before committing — not the cheapest option, the most trustworthy in-network one.
Why Opencare? He needs to confirm insurance compatibility upfront — not after booking. Opencare's matching algorithm surfaces in-network practices by default, removing his biggest research friction.
05 · Frustrations & Pain Points
🔍 Most dental directories (including his insurer's own portal) have outdated or inaccurate in-network listings. He's been burned by showing up to a practice that no longer takes his plan.
📄 Confusing insurance jargon — he can never tell if "participating provider" means what he thinks it means.
💸 Fear of surprise billing after the fact. He doesn't trust practices to be transparent about costs upfront.
🗓 Booking requires phone calls during business hours — his schedule rarely allows for that.
⏱ 54% of booking requests happen after hours (Opencare data). Marcus is squarely in this group.
06 · Example Queries to Opencare AI / Search
"Does this dentist accept Delta Dental PPO?"
"Show me dentists near the Loop open on Thursdays"
"What's covered under a new patient visit if I have insurance?"
"Is there any cost for a cleaning if I'm in-network?"
"Can I book a 12pm slot? I need to be back at work by 1:30"
Persona 02 · The Anxious Avoider

Priya Nair

A dentally anxious woman who has been putting off a visit for over two years. She's finally ready to book — but only if she can feel completely safe and unhurried before she ever walks through the door.

Age 28
Location Toronto, ON
Role UX Designer, Agency
Tech Proficiency High
Last Dental Visit 2+ years ago
01 · Context & Background

Priya had a painful extraction experience as a teenager that left her with lasting dental anxiety. She knows her teeth need attention — she's been mentioning a dull ache to her roommate for months — but every time she thinks about booking, she finds a reason to postpone.

She doesn't have employer dental insurance and pays out of pocket. Cost matters, but it's secondary to trust. She needs to feel safe with a specific dentist before she agrees to sit in the chair.

She is of South Indian heritage, culturally values warmth and personal connection in healthcare, and notices when a practice feels sterile or impersonal. No accessibility needs, though she processes decision-making slowly under stress.

02 · Tool Stack

Lives on her phone. Researches obsessively before committing — she'll spend 45 minutes reading reviews and looking at practice photos before making any move. She trusts peer recommendations on Reddit and Google over ads.

Google Search Reddit (r/askTO) Instagram iPhone / Chrome Google Maps Photos WhatsApp
Insight from product data: 42% of patients cite "a high patient rating" as a top decision factor. For Priya, this isn't optional — it's the primary filter that determines if she even considers a practice.
03 · Communication Style
Tone
Warm, tentative
Style
Detailed, exploratory
Language
Conversational
Channel pref.
Chat > Email

Priya asks soft, open-ended questions. She prefers chat interfaces over forms because they feel less clinical. She'll share context about her anxiety unprompted and responds well to empathetic, unhurried replies. She is reassured by specificity — vague answers make her more anxious.

04 · Goals & Motivations
  • 1 Find a dentist who is explicitly gentle with anxious patients — ideally mentioned in reviews by name.
  • 2 Understand exactly what will happen during her appointment before she arrives so nothing surprises her.
  • 3 Feel emotionally ready to attend — she needs a sense of control over the experience, not just a time slot.
  • 4 Address a specific concern (dull ache, possible cavity) without feeling judged for waiting so long.
Why Opencare? She discovered Opencare through a Reddit thread recommending it for finding dentists with strong reviews. The gift card offer was appealing, but what kept her was being able to read detailed patient reviews before contacting anyone.
05 · Frustrations & Pain Points
😟 58% of dental patients report some level of anxiety (Opencare survey data). Priya is in the "moderately nervous" segment and has let it stop her for 2+ years.
📸 She can't gauge a dentist's bedside manner or office atmosphere from a directory listing. She needs photos, video, and personal reviews.
🤷 Post-booking silence from the practice amplifies her anxiety. If she doesn't hear back quickly, she assumes the worst and considers cancelling.
📋 After submitting her NPR, she felt lost in the patient app — unclear on what to do next or how to check her appointment status (validated by Opencare's usability test, Feb 2026).
💬 Fear of being judged for neglecting her teeth. She needs reassurance that the practice won't lecture her.
06 · Example Queries to Opencare AI / Support
"Do you have dentists who are known for being gentle with anxious patients?"
"What actually happens during a first visit? Like step by step?"
"I submitted my request but haven't heard back — is that normal?"
"I haven't been to the dentist in 2 years, will they be judgmental about that?"
"Can I message the dentist before my appointment to ask them some questions?"
Persona 03 · The Reactive Booker

Tomás Rivera

A busy father dealing with a worsening toothache who needs a dentist today — not next week. He'll book the first available appointment that looks credible, and he'll do it from his phone at 10pm.

Age 41
Location Houston, TX
Role Warehouse Supervisor
Tech Proficiency Medium
Last Dental Visit 3+ years ago
01 · Context & Background

Tomás is a working-class father of two who hasn't prioritised dental care in years — not because he doesn't value it, but because the combination of a demanding schedule, no dental insurance through his employer, and cost uncertainty has kept him away. He is not avoiding the dentist out of fear; he simply never had a compelling enough reason to act until now.

He's been managing a dull toothache on and off for weeks with ibuprofen. This week it got significantly worse. Pain has become the trigger — he needs to act today, not plan for it.

He is bilingual (English, Spanish) and feels more comfortable with informal, plain-English communication. He has no accessibility needs but operates under significant time pressure. He is uninsured and out-of-pocket cost is a real concern, though not a blocker if the problem is serious enough.

02 · Tool Stack

Mobile-first and Google-first. He types short, direct queries and clicks the first result that looks trustworthy. He doesn't comparison-shop or read long pages — he scans for availability and proximity, then acts.

Google Search Google Maps Android / Chrome Facebook SMS / Phone calls
Insight from product data: 60% of Opencare patients want to be seen ASAP. 19% of matches explicitly indicate an urgent issue. 54% of booking requests happen after business hours — Tomás is archetypal of all three.
03 · Communication Style
Tone
Direct, terse
Style
Short, urgent
Language
Plain English
Channel pref.
SMS > Phone

Tomás writes the way he texts — short and to the point. He won't read paragraphs of instructions. He responds best to clear action prompts and dislikes being asked to fill in lengthy forms when he's in pain. If a process feels slow, he abandons it and calls a different office.

04 · Goals & Motivations
  • 1 Get an appointment as soon as possible — ideally same-day or next morning — so the pain stops interfering with his work and sleep.
  • 2 Find a dentist close to him so he doesn't lose too much time commuting on a workday.
  • 3 Get a rough sense of cost upfront — not an itemised quote, just enough to know he won't get blindsided.
  • 4 Get the problem fixed, not just triaged. He wants a dentist who will actually treat him, not just refer him elsewhere.
Why Opencare? He found Opencare via a Google search for "emergency dentist near me open tonight." The ability to book immediately online — without calling during business hours — is the platform's core value proposition for him.
05 · Frustrations & Pain Points
🕐 Availability is invisible on most dental sites. He can't tell if a dentist can actually see him today until he calls — and calling during a work shift isn't always possible.
💬 The platform currently communicates with emergency patients the same as regular patients — product data confirms there's no dedicated triage or urgency signalling in the flow.
💸 No dental insurance means cost uncertainty is a real anxiety — not knowing if a visit will cost $90 or $900 causes hesitation even when he's in pain.
📋 Long funnels with too many questions feel like barriers when he just needs to book. The emergency funnel experiment specifically identified triage question drop-off as a problem.
📍 Practices that can't actually see emergency patients appearing in results wastes his time and erodes trust in the platform.

"How much does it cost without insurance?"
"Is there a dentist within 5 miles open on Saturday?"
"My tooth has been hurting for 3 days and it's getting worse"
